# Legal AI Governance Mapping v0.1

## Purpose

This mapping explains how the Legal Domain Profile relates to public AI governance, legal-practice, adjudicative, administrative, regulatory, and rights-affecting institutional concerns.

It is intentionally high-level and should be updated as local law, court practice notes, professional guidance, public-sector AI policies, institutional rules, and technical capabilities evolve.

## Mapping table

|Current concern|Legal-domain governance response|
|-|-|
|Responsible AI use in legal or rights-affecting processes|Require verification, attribution, and accountable human responsibility before reliance|
|AI-generated or AI-assisted legal material|Classify task type, risk level, responsible actor, and reliance status before use|
|Fake citations, fabricated authorities, or hallucinated legal claims|Require independent source checking and prohibit reliance on unverified authorities|
|AI-assisted evidence summaries or record transformations|Preserve evidentiary integrity, record integrity, source references, provenance, and review pathways|
|Confidential, privileged, protected, personal, or sensitive material|Apply access controls, data minimisation, retention limits, and explicit approval rules|
|Public-sector or institutional decision support|Require accountable human ownership, disclosure assessment, contestability, and review or reconsideration pathways|
|AI policy drift|Record policy, schema, workflow, and state changes through proof-carrying change records|
|Audit overload|Use sparse, relevant, time-bounded authorisation artifacts rather than narrative-heavy compliance reports|
|Jurisdictional variation|Preserve functional requirements while mapping terminology to local law and institutional duties|
|Expired or outdated authorisation|Require expiry, review, renewal, or withdrawal conditions before continued reliance|

## Operational translation

A legal or rights-affecting institution should be able to answer:

1. Was AI used?
2. What was it used for?
3. Was the use administrative, research-support, drafting-support, evidentiary, procedural, advisory, adjudicative, regulatory, enforcement-related, or rights-affecting?
4. Was the output merely explored, or was it relied on?
5. Who authorised it?
6. Who verified it?
7. What policy or institutional rule applied?
8. What sources were checked?
9. Was disclosure required?
10. Could an affected person challenge, correct, review, appeal, or seek human reconsideration of the output or use?
11. When does the authorisation expire or require review?
12. Who remains responsible?

## Boundary

This mapping does not certify compliance. It gives institutions a structured way to discuss whether their AI workflow can prove human responsibility, verification, authority, reviewability, and scope-bounded legitimacy.
