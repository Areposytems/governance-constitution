# Legal Domain Profile v0.1

## 1. Status

Draft domain profile.  
Compatible with Governance Constitution v1.0.  
Non-normative unless explicitly adopted by an institution.

This version is written as a jurisdiction-neutral legal translation profile. It should be adapted through the laws, procedural rules, professional obligations, institutional policies, and terminology of each adopting jurisdiction.

## 2. Purpose

This profile translates the Governance Constitution into legal-domain language for institutions considering responsible AI governance in legal, adjudicative, administrative, regulatory, and quasi-legal settings.

It is intended for discussion by courts, tribunals, administrative decision-makers, legal practitioners, legal-policy bodies, legal technologists, public law scholars, regulators, ombuds institutions, legal aid bodies, and public institutions using AI in rights-affecting workflows.

This document is not legal advice, not a court rule, not a professional conduct rule, not a certification scheme, and not a compliance guarantee.

## 3. Jurisdiction-neutral interpretation

This profile is intended to travel across legal systems. Terms such as **procedural fairness**, **due process**, **natural justice**, **legal professional privilege**, **attorney-client privilege**, **protected confidentiality**, **legal practitioner**, **counsel**, **tribunal**, **court**, **administrative decision-maker**, and **rights-affecting decision** should be read according to the equivalent concepts, duties, institutions, and safeguards in the adopting jurisdiction.

Where local terminology differs, institutions should preserve the functional requirement rather than the exact wording.

## 5. Legal-domain principles

### 5.1 Human judgment and non-delegation

AI systems should remain subordinate to identifiable human responsibility.

No AI system should be treated as the author of record, responsible decision-maker, judicial or adjudicative officer, moral agent, legal professional, or substitute for accountable professional judgment in a rights-affecting legal process.

### 5.2 Verification before reliance

AI-assisted legal authorities, factual assertions, quotations, evidence summaries, chronologies, translations, procedural claims, risk classifications, and decision-support outputs should be independently verified before reliance.

Fluent output is not evidence of legal validity, factual accuracy, professional adequacy, or institutional authority.

### 5.3 Attribution and responsibility

Where AI materially contributes to legal or institutional material, the responsible human person, office, body, or institution should be identifiable.

Responsibility should not be transferred to a model, vendor, platform, tool, workflow, automated process, or undocumented institutional habit.

### 5.4 Evidentiary and record integrity

AI should not silently alter, generate, polish, compress, translate, classify, or reshape evidence or official records in a way that compromises authenticity, authorship, provenance, context, meaning, confidentiality, privilege, or reviewability.

Special care is required for witness material, sworn or affirmed statements, expert material, chronologies, translations, transcripts, documentary evidence, visual or audio evidence, metadata, official records, and material submitted by self-represented or unrepresented persons.

### 5.5 Contestability and review

Where AI materially affects a person's legal position, access to services, risk classification, procedural treatment, evidentiary record, entitlement, obligation, liberty, status, or rights-affecting decision, there should be a meaningful pathway to understand, challenge, correct, review, or seek human reconsideration of that use.

### 5.6 Auditability and recordkeeping

High-risk legal AI use should leave a proportionate audit trail, including where appropriate:

* AI system or tool used;
* user, operator, or responsible office;
* task category and action class;
* source materials or source references;
* verification steps;
* responsible reviewer or decision-maker;
* applicable policy version;
* applicable authorisation record;
* disclosure decision where applicable;
* errors identified;
* corrections made;
* expiry, review, or withdrawal conditions;
* final accountable human person, office, body, or institution.

## 6. Legal AI use categories

|Category|Examples|Governance posture|
|-|-|-|
|Low-risk administrative support|Formatting, scheduling, grammar, non-substantive document handling, internal routing|Light recordkeeping and ordinary supervision|
|Medium-risk support|Research support, draft outlines, procedural summaries, chronology preparation, translation support, public information support|Verification, attribution, human review, source retention|
|High-risk evidentiary or record use|Witness material, sworn or affirmed statements, expert material, translations, transcripts, evidence summaries, official records|Heightened verification, audit trail, provenance protection, disclosure assessment|
|High-risk rights-affecting use|Triage, risk scoring, eligibility, entitlement, enforcement, detention, sanctions, benefits, decision support, legal advice tools|Formal authorisation, accountable human approval, contestability, review and expiry conditions|
|Prohibited or exceptional use|Autonomous judicial or adjudicative decision-making, unsupervised legal determinations, fabricated authorities, fabricated facts, undisclosed evidence transformation|Not legitimate without explicit lawful authority, exceptional safeguards, reviewability, and non-delegated human accountability|

## 7. Relationship to existing law and policy

This profile does not replace legal obligations.

It is a structural translation layer intended to support, where applicable:

* procedural fairness, due process, or natural justice;
* professional responsibility and duties to the court, tribunal, client, public, or institution;
* evidentiary integrity and record integrity;
* duties of candour, honesty, accuracy, or equivalent professional obligations;
* confidentiality, legal professional privilege, attorney-client privilege, litigation privilege, or equivalent protected confidentiality;
* public-sector AI governance and administrative law safeguards;
* transparency, accountability, explainability, and recordkeeping;
* contestability, correction, appeal, review, or human reconsideration.

## 8. Adoption rule

An institution adopting this profile should define:

1. the local legal terms that correspond to this profile's jurisdiction-neutral terms;
2. the AI use categories permitted, restricted, or prohibited in that institution;
3. the human roles that remain accountable;
4. the records required before reliance or action;
5. the disclosure, contestability, review, and expiry conditions that apply;
6. the process for updating the profile when law, policy, tools, or institutional practice changes.

## 9. Closing statement

Legal institutions should not discover AI misuse only after harm has occurred.

This profile asks institutions to move verification, attribution, authorisation, record integrity, expiry, and contestability upstream before AI-assisted material enters high-trust legal, adjudicative, administrative, or rights-affecting processes.
