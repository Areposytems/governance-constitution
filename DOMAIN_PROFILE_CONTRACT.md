# Domain Profile Contract

## 0. Status

This document defines the contract by which domain profiles may translate the Governance Constitution into domain-specific language, duties, institutions, and workflows.

This contract is non-normative with respect to the constitutional core. It does not amend the Governance Constitution, the mMRM Legitimacy Kernel, or any machine-checkable schema.

Its purpose is to preserve consistency across domain profiles while allowing lawful, professional, institutional, and sector-specific translation.

---

## 1. Purpose

Domain profiles exist to make the Governance Constitution usable in specific domains without changing the constitutional core.

A domain profile may explain how constitutional concepts such as legitimacy, authorization, scope, expiry, evidence references, non-delegation, reviewability, proof-carrying change, and accountable responsibility appear within a particular field.

A domain profile must remain a translation layer. It must not become a new source of constitutional authority.

---

## 2. Definition of a Domain Profile

A domain profile is a non-normative translation document that maps the Governance Constitution into a specific field, sector, institution type, or workflow environment.

Examples may include, but are not limited to:

* legal systems
* healthcare systems
* financial systems
* property systems
* academic systems
* agentic software systems
* public administration systems
* institutional governance systems

A domain profile may describe relevant concepts, risks, roles, duties, workflows, examples, and adoption considerations for that domain.

A domain profile does not create compliance, certification, authorization, or institutional approval by itself.

---

## 3. Relationship to the Constitutional Core

The Governance Constitution remains the constitutional core.

Domain profiles must not amend, override, weaken, expand, or reinterpret the constitutional core.

The following constitutional commitments must remain invariant across all domain profiles:

* separation of powers
* non-semantic governance
* sparse legitimacy
* explicit authorization
* time-bounded legitimacy
* non-delegation
* proof-carrying change
* read-only assurance
* reviewability
* accountable responsibility
* no silent escalation
* no hidden execution path
* no domain profile as a legitimacy-binding authority

If a domain profile conflicts with the Governance Constitution, the Governance Constitution controls.

---

## 4. Permitted Functions of a Domain Profile

A domain profile may:

1. Translate constitutional terms into domain-specific language.
2. Identify domain-specific institutions, roles, duties, and workflows.
3. Map domain concepts onto existing constitutional components.
4. Explain how Legitimacy Binding Artifacts may appear in the domain.
5. Describe domain-specific examples of authorization, evidence references, scope, expiry, review, and responsibility.
6. Identify relevant risks, failure modes, and adoption considerations.
7. Provide non-binding guidance for institutional adoption.
8. Reference local law, professional duties, sector standards, or institutional policies as external considerations.
9. Suggest adoption guides, checklists, mappings, or illustrative examples.
10. Clarify that domain-specific compliance requires separate legal, professional, regulatory, or institutional review.

A domain profile may assist interpretation, but it must not bind constitutional legitimacy.

---

## 5. Prohibited Functions of a Domain Profile

A domain profile must not:

1. Amend the Governance Constitution.
2. Redefine legitimacy.
3. Create a new legitimacy-binding authority.
4. Override mMRM.
5. Replace or weaken the requirement for an applicable, unexpired Legitimacy Binding Artifact where Action Lane legitimacy is required.
6. Convert advisory, interpretive, or evidentiary outputs into authorization.
7. Treat participation, assistance, validation, review, or federation as approval unless explicitly bound by an LBA.
8. Claim legal, medical, financial, academic, regulatory, professional, or institutional compliance by itself.
9. Certify systems, vendors, institutions, or workflows.
10. Grant permission to act.
11. Create hidden execution paths.
12. Collapse human responsibility into automation.
13. Allow domain-specific urgency to bypass constitutional requirements.
14. Permit evergreen or indefinite legitimacy.
15. Treat AI-generated outputs as substitutes for accountable human judgment where human responsibility is required by law, policy, profession, or institutional duty.

---

## 6. Required Profile Structure

A domain profile should include the following sections where applicable:

1. Status
2. Purpose
3. Scope
4. Relationship to the Governance Constitution
5. Domain Translation Table
6. Domain Roles and Responsibility Mapping
7. Domain-Specific Legitimacy Questions
8. Domain-Specific Risk and Failure Modes
9. Relationship to Law, Policy, Standards, or Professional Duties
10. Adoption Considerations
11. Limits and Non-Compliance Statement
12. Closing Statement

This structure may be adapted where necessary, but every domain profile must clearly distinguish between:

* constitutional requirements
* domain translation
* adoption guidance
* illustrative examples
* external legal, professional, regulatory, or institutional duties

---

## 7. Domain Translation Table

Each domain profile should include a translation table mapping constitutional concepts into domain-specific terms.

Example structure:

| Constitutional Concept | Domain Translation                                                        | Notes                                                        |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Legitimacy             | Domain-specific authorization basis                                       | Does not mean truth, correctness, safety, or compliance      |
| LBA                    | Domain authorization artifact                                             | Must remain explicit, scoped, time-bounded, and reviewable   |
| Evidence references    | Domain evidence pointers                                                  | Evidence content remains external unless separately governed |
| Non-delegation         | Human or institutional responsibility cannot be transferred to automation | Domain law or policy may impose stricter requirements        |
| PCSU                   | Proof-carrying state or record transition                                 | Used where system state, schema, record, or workflow changes |
| CAA                    | Read-only assurance and observability                                     | Does not certify or approve                                  |

The table should clarify domain meaning without redefining constitutional authority.

---

## 8. Domain Roles and Responsibility

A domain profile should identify the roles that may appear in the domain and explain how responsibility remains accountable.

A domain profile may describe roles such as:

* accountable decision-maker
* reviewer
* approver
* practitioner
* administrator
* operator
* affected person
* institution
* external auditor
* assurance observer
* AI system or agent
* platform or infrastructure provider

A domain profile must preserve the distinction between assistance and responsibility.

AI systems, automated workflows, or agentic tools may assist, propose, analyze, draft, classify, retrieve, or prepare outputs, but they must not silently become the responsible authority for consequential action.

---

## 9. Domain-Specific Legitimacy Questions

Each domain profile should translate the core legitimacy questions into the domain.

At minimum, it should preserve the following questions:

1. Who authorized the action?
2. What exactly was allowed?
3. What was the permitted scope?
4. When does the authorization expire?
5. What evidence references support the action?
6. What system, workflow, or record changed?
7. Who reviewed or may review the action?
8. Who remains responsible?
9. What happens if the authorization is absent, expired, revoked, or out of scope?
10. How can an affected person, reviewer, institution, or assurance process inspect the authorization trail?

These questions may be expanded for domain-specific needs but must not be weakened.

---

## 10. Relationship to Adoption Guides

Domain profiles and adoption guides are distinct.

A domain profile translates the constitutional core into a domain.

An adoption guide explains how a particular institution or workflow might begin applying that profile.

A domain profile should remain broadly reusable across a field.

An adoption guide may be more specific to a setting, such as:

* court or tribunal adoption
* hospital adoption
* university adoption
* financial institution adoption
* GitHub-style agentic workflow adoption
* property management adoption
* public-sector agency adoption

Adoption guides are non-normative implementation aids. They do not amend the Constitution or the relevant domain profile.

---

## 11. Relationship to Examples

Examples are illustrative artifacts.

Examples may show how a Legitimacy Binding Artifact, proof-carrying state transition, checklist, risk-benefit matrix, governance mapping, or adoption workflow might appear in practice.

Examples must not be treated as complete implementations, compliance templates, institutional approvals, or mandatory forms.

An example should clearly state that it is illustrative only.

---

## 12. External Law, Policy, and Professional Duties

Domain profiles may reference external law, policy, regulation, professional standards, institutional duties, or ethical norms.

Such references are contextual. They do not convert the Governance Constitution into legal, medical, financial, academic, regulatory, or professional compliance.

Local adoption requires separate review by qualified domain professionals and accountable institutions.

Where external duties impose stricter requirements than the Governance Constitution, the external duty may govern the adopting institution, but it does not amend the constitutional core.

---

## 13. Compatibility Requirements

A domain profile should declare compatibility with:

* Governance Constitution version
* mMRM Legitimacy Kernel version
* relevant schema versions
* related domain-profile changelog, if applicable

Example:

```text
Compatible with:
- Governance Constitution v1.0.1
- mMRM Legitimacy Kernel v1.0
- LBA Schema v1.0
- PCSU Transition Schema v1.0
```

If compatibility changes, the domain profile should include a changelog or version note.

Silent compatibility changes are discouraged.

---

## 14. Versioning

Domain profiles should be versioned independently from the constitutional core.

A domain profile version does not imply amendment to the Governance Constitution.

Profile versioning should distinguish between:

* draft profiles
* stable profiles
* maintenance updates
* terminology updates
* adoption-guide updates
* example updates

Domain-profile updates should state whether they are semantic or non-semantic.

---

## 15. Review Standard for New Domain Profiles

Before a new domain profile is added, it should be reviewed against the following questions:

1. Does it preserve the constitutional core?
2. Does it avoid creating a new legitimacy authority?
3. Does it preserve mMRM as the legitimacy-binding kernel?
4. Does it keep AI assistance separate from accountable responsibility?
5. Does it clearly distinguish translation from compliance?
6. Does it avoid claiming certification, approval, or legal validity?
7. Does it preserve explicit authorization, scope, expiry, evidence references, reviewability, and responsibility?
8. Does it avoid silent execution paths?
9. Does it identify domain-specific roles and affected parties?
10. Does it make future adoption easier without weakening constitutional invariants?

If the answer to any of these questions is no, the profile should be revised before publication.

---

## 16. Minimal Profile Rule

A domain profile should be only as detailed as necessary to translate the constitutional core into the domain.

Profiles should prefer clarity over completeness.

Profiles should not attempt to solve every domain-specific governance, legal, ethical, technical, professional, or operational problem.

Those concerns may be handled by adoption guides, institutional policies, external standards, or future extensions.

The core profile should remain stable, readable, and reusable.

---

## 17. Closing Statement

Domain profiles allow the Governance Constitution to travel across fields without losing its constitutional identity.

They provide domain-specific language, context, and adoption pathways while preserving the same underlying legitimacy grammar:

* explicit authorization
* declared scope
* time-bounded permission
* evidence references
* non-delegation
* proof-carrying change
* read-only assurance
* reviewability
* accountable responsibility

A domain profile may translate the Constitution.

It must not rewrite it.
